The Real TCO of Big Data
  • By Vihar Chokshi
  • In financial
  • Posted Dec 3, 2016

Editors Note: This is the first part in a multi-part series on the real costs of big data.

Executive Summary

The Total Cost of Ownership of a 1PB Big Data solution over 5 years can exceed $37M1. Infrastructure requirements, licensing costs, Operations & Maintenance (O&M) costs, and growth are often timed underestimated. The biggest risk to budgetary adherence is the scarcity and wide range of salary requirements for qualified data scientists.

Market Research

We have interviewed 32 senior business and IT leaders who have implemented a Big Data Solution. Implementations ranged from small2 to large3 with varying vendors, licensing models and deployment services. Additionally, each client had a unique use case, ranging from IT only data with director level sponsorship to identifying business relevant data with high executive engagement.

In addition to the technical details associated with each implementation, we asked our clients to estimate returns with 3 different types of ROI: Hard ROI, Soft ROI and Strategic ROI. For Hard ROI, we asked them to use their revenue as the base and provide a percentage increase in revenue as a result of the solution and a decrease in costs to the business.

ACME, Inc. Overview

To normalize the technical comparisons of our 32 implementations, we set out to put each implementation into a common size and framework. Ultimately, we developed a mock company with a mock Big Data Project, called ACME, Inc.

ACME, Inc. Project Team

ACME has two teams, one is the “Business” and the other is “IT”. In our research, we found that while it was important that the business have appropriate levels of expectations of what a Big Data solution could provide, variability to the ROI could not be normalized and should therefore be excluded from calculation. For the project deployment, we chose to use the Plan, Build, Run approach to simplify the model and allocate normalized costs to coarse buckets.

Big Data Solution Requirements

Each of the 32 installations chose different solutions. On the smaller size, we found organizations tended to virtualize more, choose annual (term) licensing models, and buy commercial, off the shelf software (COTS) to manage costs and minimize risk. On the large end, we found organizations tended towards bare metal systems for reduced contention of resources, open source software with subscription licensing for support. In two cases, we found organizations choosing fully turnkey solutions where the OEM provided everything from hardware, to software, to racks and cabling for an all-in-one solution.

ACME, Inc. Solution Requirements

Based on current market trends, we chose to normalize our data by placing greater emphasis on virtualized systems and term licensing models. In our analysis, we found that the overall cost towards these solutions was lower in the short term and hence would be appropriate for a conservative ROI calculation.

Variations in daily ingestion rate and active storage capabilities varied widely, so in order to normalize our model we calculated everything in terms of 1 Petabyte system. By taking this approach, we were able to again use the lowest costs due to economies of scale to present a conservative ROI calculation.

Project Timelines

The duration of time for plan varied between 60 days for the smallest implementations to 180 for the largest and most complex. As every organization had a different methodology for project execution, we placed greater emphasis on the quality of data where sponsorship was strong and implementations that had a disciplined approach to execution.

Build time varied in a similar manner a plan time based on sponsorship and disciplined execution. Therefore, greater emphasis on build was placed on quality of data where sponsorship and discipline were strong.
